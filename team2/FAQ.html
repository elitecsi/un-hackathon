Frequently Asked Questions

How long is a typical menstrual cycle?

Answer: A menstrual cycle is counted from the first day of a woman’s period up to the first day of the next period. Typically, a menstrual cycle is 28 days long. The length of a woman’s menstrual cycle might be different month to month.

What is ovulation?

Answer: Ovulation is when the ovary releases an egg that is able to be fertilized by sperm, which causes pregnancy. Pregnancy is most likely in the three days before and up to ovulation if a woman is not on birth control.

When should I see a doctor about a period problem?

Answer: See a doctor about your period if:

  • You have gone three months without a period and are not pregnant, breastfeeding, or in perimenopause or menopause.
  • Your period is irregular (it happens more often than every 24 days or less often than every 38 days, or it lasts longer than 8 days).
  • You feel dizzy, lightheaded, weak, or tired, or you have chest pain or trouble breathing during or after your period.
  • You bleed through one or more pads or tampons every one to two hours.
  • You get a fever and feel sick after using tampons.
  • You have menstrual pain that doesn’t improve with over-the-counter pain medication.
  • Your period pain, cramps or bleeding make you miss work, school or other daily activities.
  • You get a migraine around your period or your regular migraine treatment stops working.
  • You have blood clots in your menstrual flow that are larger than a quarter.
  • You have bleeding after sex more than once.
  • You have spotting or bleeding any time in your menstrual cycle other than during your period.
  • You have bleeding after menopause.

How does my menstrual cycle affect my health?

Answer: Your hormone levels change throughout your menstrual cycle, which can cause or exacerbate health problems.

  • Anemia: Heavy menstrual bleeding is the most common cause of iron-deficiency in women. Anemia is a condition that causes you to feel tired and/or weak. Anemia happens when your blood cannot carry enough oxygen throughout your body due to the lack of iron.
  • Asthma: Your asthma symptoms may worsen during some parts of your menstrual cycle.
  • Depression: Symptoms of depression may worsen during your period. Women with a history of depression are more likely to have PMS or premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD).
  • Irritable bowel syndrome (IBS): IBS causes cramping, bloating and gas. IBS symptoms may worsen right before your period.
  • Problems getting pregnant: Certain health problems such as endometriosis, polycystic ovary syndrome, being underweight, or obesity can cause irregular periods. This can make getting pregnant more difficult.
